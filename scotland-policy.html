Scotland's Policy Context

The Policy Backdrop for Housing Digital Transformation

Understanding the national and legislative context gives the Housing Digital Transformation programme both its mandate and its constraints. These are not optional frameworks — they are the policy environment within which ERC must operate.

Policy areas: Digital, Housing, Equalities, Procurement
Key documents: Digital Scotland, Housing to 2040, PSED

Why Policy Context Shapes My Approach

I believe that framing the Housing Digital Transformation programme only in terms of technology features and delivery methodology presents an incomplete picture. ERC is a public body subject to statutory duties, national policy obligations, and procurement regulations. My delivery plan is built to work within these constraints — not treat them as obstacles to route around — and being able to articulate that clearly is central to how I approach the role.

Digital Scotland Policy

Digital Scotland Strategy 2021–2027

Scotland's national digital strategy sets the ambition for Scotland to be a world-leading digital nation. For local government, it establishes clear expectations: public services should be digital by default (while maintaining alternative channels), built around user needs, and measured by outcome rather than output.

The strategy identifies five priority outcomes:

  • Digital connectivity for all: full-fibre and mobile broadband coverage
  • A thriving digital economy and inclusive growth
  • World-class digital public services: accessible, efficient, trusted
  • A digitally skilled population and workforce
  • A secure, trusted, and ethical digital environment

ERC's own Digital Transformation Strategy 2021–2024 explicitly aligns with this national strategy. The Housing Digital Transformation programme is a direct contribution to “world-class digital public services”.

Local Government Digital Programme

The joint COSLA and Scottish Government Local Government Digital Programme supports Scottish councils in digital transformation. Key elements:

  • Shared platforms: mygov.scot citizen account, Pay360 payment processing — councils can use these rather than building their own
  • Peer learning network: Cross-council sharing of case studies, common challenges, and solutions
  • Digital standards: Alignment with Scottish Digital standards for data, accessibility, and security
  • Funding support: Targeted funding streams for priority digital investment areas, including housing
  • Scottish Digital Academy: Co-funded training for public sector digital skills

ERC participates in the LGDP network. The Housing Digital Transformation programme should engage with the network for peer learning and to access available support.

Scottish Digital Academy

The Scottish Government's public sector digital skills training organisation. The SDA provides:

  • Scottish Approach to Service Design training (5-day and shorter formats)
  • User research skills for public sector practitioners
  • Agile delivery for public sector project managers
  • Data analysis and Power BI skills
  • Digital leadership for senior managers

ERC has already sent 100+ staff through SDA programmes. The Housing Digital Transformation programme should use SDA to upskill housing staff during the programme, particularly in SAtSD and user research, enabling in-house capability post-programme.

Data Strategy for Scotland

Scotland's national data strategy sets principles for ethical, secure, and effective data use across the public sector:

  • Open by default: Data should be shared and published unless there is a reason not to
  • Ethical data use: Clear consent, transparency of purpose, avoidance of bias
  • Shared data infrastructure: Avoid duplication; use national data assets where available
  • Data as a public good: The value of public sector data should benefit citizens, not just the organisations holding it

Directly relevant to the Golden Record work, the use of IoT sensor data, and the design of the benefits measurement framework — all of which involve tenant data held by ERC.

Housing Policy

Housing to 2040 — Scotland's 25-Year Housing Strategy

The Scottish Government's 25-year housing strategy (published 2021) sets out a vision for every Scottish household to have access to a high-quality, affordable, and well-managed home by 2040. Key provisions relevant to ERC's Housing Digital Transformation:

Energy Efficiency Targets

EPC Band C by 2025 for private rented sector. EPC Band B by 2032 for social housing sector (including ERC's 3,141 units). These targets create a direct case for IoT monitoring to identify energy-inefficient properties proactively.

Tenant Participation

Scottish Secure Tenancy conditions require landlords to provide meaningful tenant participation in decisions about housing services. This is a legal mandate for co-design of the tenant portal — SAtSD with a genuine tenant panel is not just best practice, it is a statutory obligation.

Repairs Standards

Housing to 2040 reinforces Scottish secure tenants' right to a high-quality responsive repairs service. The Housing (Scotland) Act requires emergency repairs within 24 hours; urgent repairs within 3 days. Digital tracking demonstrates compliance with these statutory obligations.

Digital Telecare: Analogue Switch-Off

The national analogue telephone network will be fully switched off by the end of 2025, making all analogue telecare devices (pendant alarms, door sensors) non-functional. This is a mandatory transformation programme for all Scottish social landlords with telecare users.

ERC's exposure: Approximately 3,000 residents currently on analogue telecare, predominantly housing tenants and H&SCP service users. ERC's Digital Telecare Transition Programme began in 2023.

Relevance to Housing Digital: The telecare transition requires close coordination with the housing programme because many affected residents are tenants. The resident engagement model developed for telecare can inform the digital inclusion approach for the repairs portal.

Any tenant digital communication system (SMS, email, portal notifications) must be compatible with digital telecare integration for vulnerable residents.

Minimum Housing Quality Standards

Scottish social housing must meet the Scottish Housing Quality Standard (SHQS) and will be required to meet the Energy Efficiency Standard for Social Housing (EESSH2) — Band B by 2032.

  • Compliance is monitored by the Scottish Housing Regulator
  • SHQS includes standards for heating, insulation, and damp/mould management — directly relevant to the IoT sensor programme
  • Non-compliance can result in regulatory intervention
  • Digital repairs system enables better compliance monitoring and reporting evidence

The Housing Digital Transformation programme, by improving first-time fix rates and enabling proactive maintenance through IoT data, directly supports ERC's housing standards compliance obligations.

Equalities & Inclusion

Public Sector Equality Duty (Scotland)

The Equality Act 2010 and the Equality Act 2010 (Specific Duties) (Scotland) Regulations 2012 place specific duties on Scottish public bodies including ERC. The Public Sector Equality Duty (PSED) requires ERC to have due regard to:

  • Eliminating unlawful discrimination, harassment, and victimisation
  • Advancing equality of opportunity between persons who share a protected characteristic and those who do not
  • Fostering good relations between different groups
  • Protected characteristics: age, disability, gender, race, religion, sexual orientation, pregnancy/maternity, marriage/civil partnership
  • ERC must publish Equality Impact Assessments (EQIA) for significant service changes
  • Digital transformation of a public service is a significant service change requiring EQIA
Interview-critical point: An Equality Impact Assessment for the Housing Digital Transformation programme is a legal requirement, not optional. Include it explicitly in Phase 1 activities. The assessment will inform the digital inclusion workstream by identifying which tenant groups are most at risk of disadvantage from the digital transition.

Digital Inclusion as an Equality Issue

Digital exclusion correlates strongly with age, disability, and socioeconomic status — all of which are over-represented in social housing populations. ERC's housing tenants are therefore more likely than the general population to be digitally excluded.

  • ~10 million UK adults have very limited or no digital skills (ONS)
  • Older adults (65+) and disabled people are significantly more likely to be digitally excluded
  • Social housing tenants have below-average rates of internet access
  • Digital exclusion cannot be assumed to be temporary — some tenants will never use digital services without sustained, one-to-one support

This is why maintaining non-digital channels is not a concession — it is a legal and ethical requirement.

WCAG 2.1 AA — Legal Accessibility Requirement

Under the Public Sector Bodies (Websites and Mobile Applications) Accessibility Regulations 2018, all Scottish public sector websites and mobile applications must comply with WCAG 2.1 Level AA. This is a legal requirement with enforcement by the Equality and Human Rights Commission.

  • New services must comply from launch — not after a remediation period
  • Accessibility statements must be published for each public-facing service
  • Key WCAG 2.1 AA requirements: keyboard navigation, colour contrast ratio 4.5:1, alt text for images, captions for video, logical reading order
  • Automated testing tools (Axe, WAVE) find only ~30% of accessibility issues — manual testing with real disabled users is essential

Procurement

Public Contracts Scotland

All ERC procurement of ICT systems above the relevant threshold must follow Scottish procurement legislation, which implements the Public Contracts (Scotland) Regulations 2015.

  • Services/supplies contracts above £50,000 must be advertised on Public Contracts Scotland
  • Contracts above EU thresholds (~£213,000 for public authorities) require formal OJEU/Find-a-Tender notice
  • Minimum tender periods: 25 days (standard), 15 days (electronic submission)
  • Evaluation criteria must be pre-stated; most economically advantageous tender (MEAT) approach
  • Framework agreements (e.g. Scottish Government Digital Technology frameworks) can significantly shorten procurement timelines

Housing ICT system procurement (Phase 2) must be planned into the programme timeline from the start. Allow 12–16 weeks minimum for a compliant procurement process.

Open Standards & Avoiding Vendor Lock-In

Scottish Government procurement policy explicitly requires public bodies to consider open standards and avoid creating dependency on a single vendor's proprietary ecosystem.

  • Require open APIs (REST/JSON) as a minimum in all housing ICT procurement
  • Data portability: ensure data can be exported in open formats at contract end
  • Avoid proprietary integration methods that tie ERC to a specific vendor's product ecosystem
  • Software licence portability: avoid licences that prevent migration to alternative platforms
  • Source code escrow: for custom software, ensure source code is held in escrow in case of vendor failure

Include open standards requirements in the procurement specification for the housing ICT platform and mobile workforce management tool.
