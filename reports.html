ERC Specific Documents

1
ERC Digital Transformation Strategy 2021–2024
East Renfrewshire Council, 2021

The core strategic document establishing ERC's three-front approach to digital transformation: Customer Experience, Workforce Productivity, and Business Systems Modernisation. Establishes the MAP (Modern, Ambitious Programme) framework with six departmental delivery programmes.

Key Findings
  • Three strategic fronts provide a coherent framework for prioritising and aligning all digital investment
  • MAP Programme governance model creates clear accountability and cross-departmental coordination
  • Digital Champions network (30+) established as the internal change agent model
  • Investment in SAtSD capability through Scottish Digital Academy: 100+ staff trained
  • MyEastRen citizen portal and Goss CXM as the citizen-facing delivery platforms
  • M365 (including Teams, SharePoint, Power BI) as the staff-facing productivity platform
Why this matters to me: This is my foundational document for understanding ERC. I need to demonstrate command of the three-front structure and the MAP programme to show I understand ERC's strategic context — not just the technology layer sitting on top of it.

2
Housing Asset Management Plan (HAMP) 2024–2026
East Renfrewshire Council, 2024

ERC's strategic housing asset management document covering the 3,141-unit social housing stock. Sets investment priorities for planned and reactive maintenance, energy efficiency improvements, new build programme, and digital service improvement.

Key Findings
  • 3,141 housing units managed by ERC across East Renfrewshire
  • 171 new housing units to be completed by 2028 as part of the new build programme
  • EPC Band B target by 2032 (Scottish Government social housing energy efficiency standard)
  • Digital Repairs project formally scoped as a key investment priority: improving repairs productivity and tenant experience
  • Planned maintenance investment priorities identified to reduce reactive demand over time
  • Tenant satisfaction with repairs identified as a key performance area requiring improvement
Why this matters to me: The HAMP is the business case for this role. I reference it to show I understand the housing context — not just the digital context. The figures — 3,141 units, EPC Band B by 2032, 171 new units — are the backdrop to every investment decision in the programme.

3
Digital Repairs Project Scoping Document
East Renfrewshire Council, 2024

The internal project scoping document for ERC's Housing Digital Repairs programme. Sets out the vision for enhancing repairs service productivity through digital enablement, improving both the tenant experience and the efficiency of the housing repairs workforce.

Key Findings
  • Programme objective: enhance productivity of the Housing Repairs service and improve tenant experience
  • 20-month delivery window established
  • Key workstreams: tenant-facing portal, mobile workforce management, scheduling optimisation, system integration
  • Sits within MAP programme governance structure, reporting to Housing programme steering group
  • Identified dependency on housing ICT system modernisation (legacy replacement)
  • Benefits to be measured: channel shift, first-time fix rate, tenant satisfaction, staff time savings
Why this matters to me: This document defines the scope of the role I'm applying for. I need to be clear on both what is directly in scope (productivity, tenant experience) and what sits alongside it (legacy system modernisation) — understanding the boundaries demonstrates strategic awareness rather than a narrow delivery focus.

Scottish Government

4
Digital Scotland Strategy 2021–2027
Scottish Government, 2021

Scotland's national digital strategy setting the ambition for Scotland to become a world-leading digital nation. Covers public services, connectivity, the digital economy, digital skills, and the role of public sector organisations in driving digital transformation.

Key Findings
  • Public sector digital transformation as a strategic national priority, not optional improvement
  • Shared platforms (mygov.scot, Pay360) as the foundation for consistent citizen digital experience
  • Scottish Approach to Service Design (SAtSD) as the mandated design methodology for public services
  • Scottish Digital Academy to build public sector digital capability at scale
  • Local Government Digital Programme (COSLA/Scottish Government joint) supporting council transformation
  • Emphasis on digital inclusion: no one left behind by digital transformation
Why this matters to me: I frame ERC's Housing Digital programme within the national Digital Scotland context. Using SAtSD is not just best practice — it aligns with national policy. Integrating with mygov.scot is not just convenient — it is the Scottish Government's strategic platform for public sector citizen access, and choosing it is the right decision architecturally as well as politically.

5
Housing to 2040 — Scottish Government Housing Strategy
Scottish Government, 2021

Scotland's 25-year housing strategy, setting out the vision for housing that is affordable, high quality, and available in the right places. Covers supply, quality, affordability, fuel poverty, and the role of social housing in delivering national wellbeing outcomes.

Key Findings
  • Ambition for all homes to reach EPC Band C by 2025 (rented sector) and Band B by 2032 (social sector)
  • Ending fuel poverty as a statutory target — digital data and IoT as tools for early identification
  • Quality housing management, including responsive repairs, as a central component of tenant experience
  • Digital enablement of housing management services as a means of improving both efficiency and tenant outcomes
  • Tenant participation in service design is a Scottish statutory requirement under Scottish Secure Tenancy conditions
  • New housing supply to include digital infrastructure as standard
Why this matters to me: I connect the Housing Digital programme to Housing to 2040 specifically to show that the digital investment serves long-term housing quality and sustainability goals. EPC targets strengthen the case for expanding ERC's IoT sensor pilot; tenant participation requirements mean SAtSD co-design is legally as well as practically the right approach.

Sector Bodies & Research

6
CivTech Housing Innovation Report
CivTech Scotland / Scottish Government, 2023

CivTech is Scotland's government technology accelerator, connecting public sector challenges with technology companies. Housing challenges have featured in multiple CivTech cohorts, producing innovative solutions for repairs, tenant communication, and maintenance management.

Key Findings
  • AI-powered repair diagnosis: tenants describe problems in plain language, system categorises and routes to correct trade
  • Predictive maintenance algorithms trained on historical repairs data and sensor readings
  • Video triage solutions: tenant films the problem before a contractor is dispatched, reducing unnecessary visits
  • Natural language processing for repairs request analysis and demand forecasting
  • Procurement pathway: CivTech graduates can be procured by Scottish public bodies through established routes
Why this matters to me: I'm aware of the Scottish innovation ecosystem and the CivTech route specifically. Solutions developed through CivTech are procured via Public Contracts Scotland — a route I would consider for innovative repairs technology where a traditional full tender process would be disproportionate to the scope.

7
SFHA Digital Housing Survey 2023
Scottish Federation of Housing Associations, 2023

The Scottish Federation of Housing Associations (SFHA) annual survey of digital maturity across Scottish social landlords, including both local authorities and housing associations. The 2023 edition provides the most current benchmarking data on digital adoption in the Scottish social housing sector.

Key Findings
  • Only 38% of Scottish social landlords had a fully operational tenant self-service repairs portal in 2023 — significant room for ERC to become a sector leader
  • Mobile workforce management adoption at 45% — growing rapidly, driven by post-COVID expectations
  • Biggest barriers to digital adoption: legacy system constraints, staff resistance, and lack of dedicated programme leadership
  • Digital channel shift for repairs averaging 22% across sector — ERC's target of 30%+ by month 18 is achievable but ambitious
  • Tenant satisfaction with digital repairs processes consistently higher than phone-only services where portals are well-designed
Why this matters to me: I use SFHA benchmarking data to contextualise ERC's ambitions. A 30% digital channel shift target is ahead of the sector average — which I think reflects the right level of ambition. The barriers the survey identifies — legacy systems, staff resistance, programme leadership — are exactly the risks I address explicitly in my delivery plan.

8
COSLA / Scottish Government Local Government Digital Programme — Progress Report
COSLA / Scottish Government, 2023

Joint progress report on the Local Government Digital Programme, the shared initiative between COSLA (Convention of Scottish Local Authorities) and the Scottish Government to support councils in digital transformation. Provides an overview of what councils are doing, what support is available, and where progress has been made.

Key Findings
  • Significant variation in digital maturity across Scottish councils — ERC is consistently in the more advanced cohort
  • Shared platforms and peer learning accelerate delivery: councils that engage with the LGDP network progress faster
  • Housing and social care identified as priority service areas for digital investment at national level
  • Funding streams available through the programme for qualifying digital projects — relevant to procurement planning
  • Scottish Digital Academy training co-funded through the programme — ERC has benefited significantly
Why this matters to me: I understand the national support ecosystem available to ERC and would actively use it. LGDP peer learning means ERC does not need to solve every problem from scratch — it can draw on the experience of councils that have already navigated similar programmes. That is a resource I would build into the delivery approach from day one.

Audit & Standards

9
Audit Scotland: Digital Transformation in Scottish Councils
Audit Scotland, 2021

Audit Scotland's 2021 review of digital transformation programmes across Scottish local authorities. Provides independent analysis of what has worked, what has failed, and what governance and management disciplines make the difference between successful and unsuccessful digital programmes.

Key Findings
  • Unclear ownership is the single most common cause of digital programme failure in Scottish councils
  • Benefits not baselined: Most programmes could not demonstrate measurable improvement because they failed to measure the “before” state
  • Change management as afterthought: Technology deployed without adequate staff engagement consistently fails to deliver intended productivity gains
  • Procurement delays are the most common cause of timeline slippage in council digital programmes
  • Councils that invested in digital skills (user research, agile delivery) before starting major programmes achieved significantly better outcomes
  • Resident/tenant involvement in design correlates strongly with better adoption rates and higher satisfaction
Why this matters to me: The Audit Scotland findings are directly reflected in my delivery plan — named SRO from day one, benefits baseline captured at Month 2, change management as a dedicated workstream, procurement starting in Phase 1. I have specifically designed the plan to address the failure modes Audit Scotland identified across Scottish council digital programmes.

10
GDS Service Standard (UK Government Digital Service)
Government Digital Service, 2019 (updated regularly)

The UK Government Digital Service Standard sets out 14 criteria that government services must meet to be delivered digitally. While Scottish public services follow SAtSD rather than the GDS standard as their primary framework, the GDS standard is a useful comparative reference that shares many of the same principles and adds specific technical and operational requirements.

Key Findings / Key Points
  • Understand users and their needs (Point 1) — requires user research, not assumed requirements
  • Solve a whole problem for users (Point 2) — end-to-end service design, not just digitising a form
  • Provide a joined up experience across all channels (Point 3) — phone, web, and in-person must be coherent
  • Make the service simple to use (Point 4) — WCAG 2.1 AA, plain language, inclusive design
  • Make sure everyone can use the service (Point 5) — digital inclusion as a design requirement, not an add-on
  • Have a plan for being offline (Point 11) — what happens when the portal is down
  • Measure performance (Point 10) — four key metrics: completion rate, user satisfaction, cost per transaction, uptake
Why this matters to me: I reference the GDS Service Standard to structure the tenant portal requirements and give technical partners a shared language. Points 5 (solve the whole problem for users) and Point 10 (define what success looks like) map directly to the KPIs and digital inclusion strategy in my delivery plan — and they are points I can cite by number when discussing design decisions.
